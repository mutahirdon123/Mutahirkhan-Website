Giant Tube Worm:

Scientific name: Riftia pachyptila They are found at the depth of 8000m near the hydrothermal vents. They have no mouth and no digestive tract. They depend on bacteria that live inside them for their food. This type of mutually beneficial relationship between two organisms is known as symbiosis. The bacteria actually convert the chemicals from the hydrothermal vents into organic molecules that provide food for the worm. These organisms contain Bright red plume. This plume exchange organic compounds with vent environment and provide nutrients to bacteria that live inside them.

Clams and crustaceans:

They are found at the depth of 8000m near the hydrothermal vents. These organisms were getting their food directly from the vents themselves. This process is known as chemosynthesis. Hydrothermal vents support unique ecosystems, Clams includes: Vent clams (Calyptogena gigas) are filter feeders. Vesicomyid clams (Vesicomya) are symbiotic bacteria-rich. Crustaceans includes: Vent crabs (Bythograea thermydron) are scavengers. Hydrothermal vent shrimp (Rimicaris exoculata) are chemosynthetic. Vent lobsters (Munidopsis) are opportunistic feeders. They are adapted to: 1. Chemosynthesis - symbiotic bacteria convert chemicals to energy 2. Thermal tolerance - survive in hot, chemical-rich environments 3. Symbiotic relationships - mutualistic bacteria provide nutrients 4. Slow metabolism - conserve energy in food-scarce environments

Pyrococcus furiosus:

Pyrococcus furiosus is a hyperthermophilic archaeon that thrives in extreme environments, particularly hydrothermal vents. It can grow at temperatures around 100°C (212°F) and is known for its unique metabolic capabilities, including the ability to ferment sugars and utilize sulfur. This microorganism is of interest for its potential applications in biotechnology, especially in enzyme production for processes that require high temperatures. Its enzymes, like DNA polymerases, are valuable for molecular biology techniques.This archaeon has symbiotic relationships with other microorganisms, contributing to nutrient cycling in extreme conditions.
