Shewaneua aneidensis

Shewaneua aneidensis: is a psychrophilic bacterium belonging to the Gammaproteobacteria class, primarily found in deep-sea environments at depths of around 1000 meters or more. It is rod-shaped and motile, known for its diverse metabolic capabilities, including the reduction of metal ions and organic compounds. Adapted to cold and high-pressure conditions, it possesses enzymatic systems optimized for low temperatures and membrane fluidity adaptations. While specific symbiotic associations are not well-documented.

Alteromonas macleodii

Alteromonas macleodii: is a marine bacterium commonly found at depths around 1000 to 4000 meters. It is rod-shaped, motile, and heterotrophic, playing a crucial role in marine carbon cycling. While not strictly a psychrophile, it is adapted to cold temperatures typical of deep-sea environments, with enzymes that function efficiently in low temperatures and mechanisms to withstand high pressure. A. macleodii can also form symbiotic associations with various marine organisms, contributing to nutrient cycling and the health of marine ecosystems.

Oxyrrhis marina:

Oxyrrhis marina:• A dinoflagellate, classified under the phylum Dinoflagellata.• Structural Features: Oxyrrhis marina has a distinctive oval shape and is characterized by two flagella, which aid in movement. It has a cell wall made of cellulose and is often covered with scales or plates.• Bioluminescence: Oxyrrhis marina exhibits bioluminescence, which is common among many dinoflagellates. This bioluminescence is thought to play a role in predator avoidance and communication.• Biofilm: While it does not form traditional biofilms, Oxyrrhis marina can aggregate in high concentrations, especially in nutrient-rich waters, which can lead to blooms.• Depth: Typically found in surface waters and can thrive in various marine environments, including coastal areas.• Survival Mechanisms: Its flagella allow for motility, enabling it to move towards light (phototaxis) and nutrients.• It can also switch between heterotrophic and autotrophic modes, allowing it to survive in different nutrient conditions.• Type: Oxyrrhis marina is not specifically categorized as a halophile, psychrophile, or basophile; it is generally adaptable to various salinity and temperature conditions.• Symbiosis: While not typically known for symbiotic relationships, it can interact with other marine organisms, and its blooms can impact local ecosystems.

Acantharia acomthodes:

(1) Part of the radiolarian group, similar to Acantharia leptoceras. (2) Structural Features: Features a siliceous skeleton with a complex, spiny structure. The central capsule is prominent, surrounded by radiating spines that enhance buoyancy and prey capture. (3) Bioluminescence: Like many Acantharia, they may possess bioluminescent properties, useful for attracting prey or deterring predators. (4) Biofilm: Acantharia generally do not form biofilms in the traditional sense, but their tests can aggregate under specific conditions, contributing to marine sediment. (5) Depth: Typically found at depths ranging from 200 to 3000 meters. (6) Survival Mechanisms: The intricate silica skeleton provides support and aids in buoyancy, allowing them to thrive in nutrient-rich deeper waters. (7) Type: Psychrophiles; adapted to cold deep-sea environments. (8) Symbiosis: Potential symbiotic relationships with photosynthetic organisms (lik algae) can enhance their nutrient intake.

Atlantic Hagfish:

Scientific Name: Myxine glutinosa Structural Features: Hagfish have a long, eel-like body, usually reaching lengths of about 20 to 40 inches (50 to 100 cm). They possess a cartilaginous skull and lack true vertebrae. Notable for their slime glands, which secrete a mucous substance for defense. Bioluminescence: Atlantic hagfish do not exhibit bioluminescence. Biofilm: Hagfish may interact with microbial communities in their habitat but do not form biofilms themselves. Depth: Found at depths ranging from 100 to 2,000 meters (328 to 6,561 feet) in the North Atlantic Ocean. Survival Mechanisms: They are highly adapted to deep-sea conditions, including high pressure and low light. Their ability to produce large amounts of slime helps them evade predators and aids in feeding by engulfing prey. Type: Atlantic hagfish are often considered euryhaline, as they can tolerate a range of salinity levels, but they thrive in cold, deep-sea environments. Symbiosis: While not symbiotic, hagfish play a role in the marine ecosystem as scavengers, feeding on dead and decaying matter, thus contributing to nutrient cycling.

Chambered Nautilus:

* Scientific Name: Nautilus pompilius * Structural Features: The chambered nautilus has a distinctive coiled shell divided into chambers, which aids in buoyancy control. It has soft, tentacle-like appendages that surround its head, used for locomotion and capturing prey. * Bioluminescence: Chambered nautiluses do not exhibit bioluminescence. * Biofilm: They do not form biofilms but may interact with microorganisms in their environment. • Depth: Found at depths ranging from 200 to 1,000 meters (656 to 3,280 feet) in the Indo-Pacific region. * Survival Mechanisms: The shell's buoyancy helps them navigate the water column and avoid predators. They can regulate the gas and liquid in their shell chambers to ascend or descend. * Type: Chambered nautiluses prefer warm, tropical waters and are not classified as halophiles, psychrophiles, or basophiles. * Symbiosis: They have a role in the marine ecosystem as scavengers and predators, feeding on small fish and crustaceans.

oarfish:

The oarfish is a long, slender deep-sea fish known for its impressive length, sometimes reaching over 30 feet (9 meters). It has a distinctive, ribbon-like body and is typically silvery with a reddish dorsal fin. Oarfish are rarely seen by humans, as they inhabit deep waters, usually at depths of 200 to 1,000 meters. They are often associated with oceanic myths and legends, sometimes thought to be sea serpents. Despite their size, oarfish are not considered dangerous to humans, feeding primarily on plankton and small fish.

Giant Isopode:

Scientific name: Bathynomus giganteus It is the largest known members of the isopod family, a group of crustaceans closely related to shrimps and crabs. It is found in the depth of 2140m. The enormous size of the giant isopod is a result of a phenomenon known as deep sea gigantism. The reason for this size remains a mystery, although some researchers believe it may be an adaptation to help the animal deal with the enormous pressures. Food is extremely scarce at these great depths, so the isopod has adapted to eat what ever happens to fall from above.This includes bodies of dead whale, squids etc.

Giant Squid

Scientific name: Architeuthis dux The giant squid is a mollusk and is member of the cephalopod class, which includes the octopus and other squids. It is one of the world’s largest animal and is known to reach a length of up to 60 feet. They are usually found at the depth of 1175m. They feed on deep sea fish and others squids. Giant squid’s body is composed of a mantle, eight arms, and two longer tentacles. Tentacles contain suction cup which is surrounded by sharp, serrated edges that help the squid hold on to its prey. Large eyes are an important adaptation that helps the squid locate food in the darkness of the deep ocean.

Gulper Eel:

Scientific name: Eurypharynx pelecanoides It’s mouth is much larger than the eel’s body. The end of the tail is tipped with a light-producing organ known as a photophore. Through a process known as bioluminescence, the photophore glows pink and can give off occasional red flashes. The large mouth may be an adaptation to allow the eel to eat a wider variety of prey when food is scarce. It is found at the depth of 1800m. They feed on small squids.

Deep Sea Anglerfish:

Scientific name: Melanocetus johnsoni The angler gets its name from the elongated dorsal spine that supports a light-producing organ known as a photophore. Through a chemical process known as bioluminescence, this photophore can produce a blue-green light. The anglerfish uses its illuminated lure as its hunts for prey. They feed on lanternfish, dragonfish, viperfish, squids, gulper eels and dead organisms. They have ability to extend both it’s jaw and stomach to an incredible size to store food and use it during scarce of food in deep sea. They have ability to hide in dark. They have unusual method of reproduction.The male angler has small hook teeth, which it uses to attach itself to the female. The two become fused together and their blood vessels join as one. The male will spend the rest of its life joined to the female like a parasite, getting all of his nourishment from her body. A female can carry up to six males on her body at a time. They are found at the depth of 1000-2000m.
